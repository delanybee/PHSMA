The Invisible Network

What 227,000 miles of buried oil pipeline says about American infrastructure

Beneath American farmland, suburbs, and rivers run more than 227,000 miles of pipeline carrying crude oil, gasoline, and other hazardous liquids. Laid end to end, the network would circle the Earth nine times. Nearly all of it is invisible to the public it runs beneath. What follows is an accounting of that network: when it was built, who operates it, and what its age means for the land and water above it.

The Boom Nobody Watched

Between 2004 and 2019, the shale revolution drove the largest oil infrastructure expansion in American history, adding 34,000 miles of crude pipeline in a single decade. The expansion drew little public attention. The controversies that did surface, Keystone XL and Dakota Access among them, concerned individual lines while the network itself grew by thousands of miles each year.

In July 2010, midway through the boom, a ruptured pipeline released more than 840,000 gallons of crude into Michigan’s Kalamazoo River, the largest inland oil spill in United States history. Cleanup costs exceeded one billion dollars. The pipe had been installed in 1969. That year matters to everything that follows.

Built Before the Rules Existed

The Environmental Protection Agency was created in 1970. The Clean Water Act followed in 1972, and the first comprehensive federal safety law for hazardous liquid pipelines did not pass until 1979. By then, much of the network was already in service: 88,058 miles of today’s active pipeline, 39.2 percent of the whole, was installed before the EPA existed. That pipe was built to the engineering and regulatory standards of its era, and it remains in operation.

The Shape of the Network

The network’s age profile records two construction eras. The first came at mid-century, when postwar demand drove rapid building under minimal federal oversight. Construction slowed through the 1980s and 1990s, then surged again during the shale years of the 2010s. Five decades of advances in metallurgy, welding, corrosion control, and regulation separate the two eras. Pipe from both is in service today, side by side in the same network.

What Is Not Being Replaced

One might expect the oldest pipe to be retired at a meaningful rate. The data show otherwise. Between 2017 and 2024, operators added nearly 22,000 miles of new pipeline while pre-1970 mileage declined by roughly 4,500. At that rate, retirement of the pre-EPA network would extend more than a century into the future. New construction has expanded the system; it has not replaced it.

When the Pipe Fails

Aging pipe fails in documented, recurring ways. Since 2010, operators have reported 5,560 incidents to federal regulators through the end of 2024: ruptures, leaks, and releases into fields and waterways. Corrosion, the failure mode most closely tied to age, remains among the leading causes.

Named in the Record

The federal records identify each operator by name. They record the barrels released and the barrels recovered, and the distance between those figures varies widely across the industry. A small number of companies account for a disproportionate share of the volume spilled since 2010, and several of them operate systems where a significant portion of active mileage belongs to the network’s oldest decades.

The record of this infrastructure is public. Every operator, every rupture, every barrel released and every barrel recovered is filed with the federal government and available to anyone willing to look. The pipelines will keep working, mostly, the way aging infrastructure always does: silently, until the day one of them does not. What the record asks is only that someone read it.

How we reported this

Annual report data: PHMSA Form 7000-1.1, Hazardous Liquid Annual Report, Parts A–E and I (2017–2024). Available at phmsa.dot.gov. Mileage totals and installation-decade breakdowns aggregated nationally across all operators and states. Rows with unknown installation decade excluded from age analyses (approx. 1.6% of total in 2024).

Mileage summary: PHMSA Hazardous Liquid Annual Statistics (haz_liquid_annual_stat.xls, 2004–2024). Used for commodity-level mileage trends.

Incident data: PHMSA Hazardous Liquid Accident Database (tab-delimited files covering 2002–2009 and 2010–present). Complete years 2002–2024 only (8,590 incidents; 5,560 from 2010–2024). Cause categories in the 2002–2009 file normalized to match the 2010–present schema. Release volumes reported in gallons in the older file converted to barrels (42 gal/bbl). Operator rankings use unintentional release barrels as reported; two multi-entity operators (Enterprise Products Partners, Chevron Pipelines) combined from separately filed subsidiaries.

Note: This analysis covers hazardous liquid pipelines only (crude oil, refined petroleum products, highly volatile liquids, CO₂, and ethanol). Natural gas pipelines are a separate PHMSA reporting system and are not included.
